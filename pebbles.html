Pebbles alleviate the challenges of being a young parent and a teenager through the support of mentors, access to basic resources, and scheduling changes with their partner.


BACKGROUND

Teens who are already parents are at the highest risk for unplanned pregnancy. They often face social isolation, stigma and mental health issues at higher rates than their peers. Pregnant and parenting teens face many barriers to enrolling, attending, and succeeding in school, such as discrimination, the challenge of juggling schoolwork with parenting responsibilities, and lack of access to child care, transportation, and other critical services.

Problem #1

Teen parents are unaware of assistance programs that are available of no cost for her and child.

Food assistance such as food stamps and WIC are federal programs for pregnant women and children up to age 5. However, it is only a short-term solution. Case managers are provided to the teen by school staff or through a community program and/or regular home visits during and after pregnancy, they serve to connect teens to medical and social services present in the community. A case manager meets with a teen parent a minimum of twice a month, either at home or at school.  ​ It leads to this question: What if I don't have a case manager? What happens? Who do I reach out for help?.

Problem #2

Teen parents are often isolated from their community.

Teen moms often get kicked out of their homes, but they’re not allowed to stay in homeless shelters because it’s “a child with a child.” ​

Problem #3

Conflicting schedules occur while co-parenting.

Co-parenting after a separation can create tension to the relationship. From our research, we found out that teen mothers feel it is important to have her children's father present in raising the child, despite being separated.  ​ It leads to this question: What if I don't have a case manager? What happens? Who do I reach out for help?.

How might we support young parents to develop basic self-sufficiency skills so they will be able to transition to independent living and access resources without the assistance of a case manager and strengthen their capabilities as parents?

Persona

Sophia is our primary persona because she has the most needs - She is at home taking care of her child while her partner is working part-time. She relies on need-based assistance and wants to be a self-sufficient mother. She doesn't have much financial support from family and because she only meets with her case manager twice a month, she needs to be looking for other resources to meet her other needs. 

Tyrone is our secondary persona as his needs are less urgent than Sophia's. Since his working hours are unstable, often times he would miss his daughter's milestones. He wants to be more involved in her child's day instead of getting limited updates from his partner. Scheduling changes may come in the way of unexpected situations.

Initial Sketches

IDEA #1: Connect young parents to support groups that could provide basic necessities according to their children's age.
PROS: This idea would inform young parents availability of resources nearest to their location
Why it didn't work: We assumed diapers were given for free within a certain amount. However, what we learned was Individual institutions have their own set of rules in order to get supplies. For example, 10hrs of volunteer work is accumulated into points, which is then calculated and can be redeemed as diapers (for eg.) Also, it is not in the user's mental model to track their resources.

IDEA #2: Reaching a goal as co-parents
PROS: This idea would keep young parents accountable and have a shared responsibility of purchasing a need that they share (eg. buying a stroller)
Why it didn't work: This idea touches the topic of money which is a sensitive topic, and can be unfair when the individual does not have any income.There is uncertainty in how much money is earned by both parents.

IDEA #3: Connect young parents to other young parents for support
PROS: This idea would keep young parents accountable and have a shared responsibility of purchasing a need that they share (eg. buying a stroller)
Why it didn't work: Young parents don't have the time to "chat" nor talk to other young parents. They have dire need which is to prioritize where to get basic resources for her child.
